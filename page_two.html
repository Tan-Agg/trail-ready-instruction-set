Step 2 - Prepare Your Load

Strategic layering is more important when packing a backpack than cramming it.Consider it similar to cordwood stacking: stable, level rows from bottom to top, with each gap purposefully filled. When done correctly the pack moves with your body, the load shifts to your hips and your center of gravity remains low. Each of the five zones below represents a principle: once you get the sequence you won't ever overpack again. Place everything on the floor and arrange the items according to zones before you begin.

Golden Rules before you pack:

  • › Place heavy objects in the middle of your hips and shoulders and press them against your spine.
  • › There are no uneven loads and the pack hangs straight and balanced left to right.
  • › Fill every gap : Gear shifts and hurt hips on the path are caused by loose space.
  • › Things that are required on trail are placed in the lid or on top and they are never buried at the bottom.
  • › Side drift is caused by uneven weight, thus external attachments must be symmetrical.

Step 3 - The Zone Packing System : General Guidelines for every Backpack

d2d. Brain / Lid Headlamp · map · phone · sunscreen · emergency ID TThe easiest to reach area is where you should always store sun protection, emergency identification and navigational aids. Before you move, fully zip. As a backup, keep cash and identification in a ziploc bag.
c2c. Top Zone Rain jacket · snacks · first aid · water tablets · bug spray Things you need quickly and continuously. Rain gear is always on top, and the weather might change in a matter of minutes. First aid needs to be accessible in a matter of seconds. First aid should never be hidden beneath other equipment.
e2e. External Attachments Trekking poles · tent poles · sleeping mat · wet gear Make use of daisy-chain loops and compression straps. In order to prevent inner gear from being soaked, wet or soiled goods should go here. Keep both sides in balance. Nothing should be left hanging that weighs more than 0.5 kg.
2b. Core Zoneb Tent · food · stove · fuel · water filter · bear canister The heaviest objects press against your spine, shifting the weight to your hips rather than your back. To maximize space, pack the bear canister upright. Fill in all the gaps. Fill up any spaces with fleece or socks.
2a. Bottom Zonea Sleeping bag · sleeping pad · camp shoes · base layers A soft and stable basis is produced by being bulky but light. Use a compression sack but never keep it compressed for an extended period of time since this will permanently harm the insulating loft. A wet sleeping bag should never be compressed.

Step 4 - Food & Water Planning

Water

Always keep two to three liters with you. Never drink straight from streams and always carry a water filter or purification pills. Store bottles in your side pockets for convenience. trail access. On hot days, electrolyte pills are helpful.

Don't wait until it's empty and refill at every known source.

Food

Steer clear of perishables and canned goods. Top choices: Jerky, trail mix, energy bars, and freeze-dried meals. Hiking uses a lot more calories than daily living, so pack more than you might think. Aim for 2,500-4,500 kcal per day.

At night, keep all food in a bear canister.
